# Case Study — Internship at XAN IT / Quantum Campus
**Basel, Switzerland · June 2025**

---

## Context

XAN IT is a specialized IT services company operating within the Quantum Campus in Basel, Switzerland — an innovation hub at the frontier of quantum computing research and applied technology. The campus brings together researchers, engineers, and infrastructure teams working in one of the most technically demanding environments in modern computing.

I joined XAN IT as an IT intern in June 2025, embedded directly within the campus's IT operations. The team was small — four people in total — which meant that from day one, responsibilities were real, the margin for error was real, and the trust placed in me was equally real.

---

## The Challenge

Running IT infrastructure for a quantum computing campus is fundamentally different from a standard corporate environment. The requirements are exacting: research workstations must be configured to precise specifications, server uptime is critical, communication systems must be reliable around the clock, and hardware procurement decisions have direct consequences on research timelines.

Within a team of four, there is no luxury of a dedicated specialist for every domain. Each person needs to cover ground across server administration, user hardware, telephony, containerized services, and documentation. The infrastructure itself spanned both physical on-premises servers and cloud or virtualized environments, adding another layer of complexity to daily operations.

The challenge I was handed was not a narrow, supervised task list. It was: *here is the infrastructure, here are the users, here are the needs — figure it out.*

---

## My Role & Responsibilities

Despite being an intern, I operated with a level of autonomy and trust that went well beyond what is typically afforded to someone in that position. The majority of my tasks were handled independently, from initial diagnosis through to resolution, without requiring constant supervision or hand-holding. This was not circumstantial — it reflected the team's confidence in my ability to take ownership and deliver.

My responsibilities spanned five key areas:

### 1. Infrastructure Maintenance
I actively participated in the maintenance of the Quantum Campus's IT infrastructure. This meant monitoring system health, identifying potential failure points, and performing hands-on interventions on both physical servers and virtualized environments. When something broke or risked breaking, I was the one investigating and resolving it — often autonomously and under time pressure.

### 2. Server & Telephony Installation and Maintenance
I installed and maintained servers across the campus, configuring operating systems, network interfaces, services, and storage. In parallel, I managed phone systems and workstations — ensuring that the people doing cutting-edge quantum research had reliable, properly configured tools to work with. Each deployment was an exercise in precision: the wrong configuration in a research environment can cascade into larger disruptions.

### 3. Hardware Assembly & Procurement
I assembled computers from components and took part in managing IT hardware purchases. This involved evaluating technical specifications, sourcing appropriate components for the use cases on campus, and coordinating the acquisition process. Making sound procurement decisions in an innovative environment meant understanding both the immediate need and the direction the campus was heading technically.

### 4. Docker Container Deployment
I deployed Docker containers and managed clusters to support services running on campus infrastructure. This was one of the areas where I had to learn the fastest — container orchestration, networking between services, volume management, and making sure deployments were stable and reproducible. Every deployment was an independent challenge I worked through from documentation, experimentation, and problem-solving.

### 5. Technical Documentation
I wrote technical documentation covering the infrastructure, configurations, and operational procedures I worked on. Good documentation in a small IT team is not optional — it is what allows the next person to pick up where you left off without losing institutional knowledge. I took this seriously, producing clear and structured references for the systems I managed.

---

## Learning on the Spot

One of the defining characteristics of this internship was that virtually none of the skills I applied were things I had formally learned beforehand. Docker container deployment, server rack configuration, telephony infrastructure, hardware procurement workflows, cluster management — I picked up each of these on the job, in a live environment, with real consequences.

This kind of learning is categorically different from classroom or tutorial-based training. There is no safety net. When you're configuring a server that researchers depend on, or deploying a containerized service that needs to stay up, the feedback loop is immediate and unforgiving. I had to be resourceful — reading documentation thoroughly, testing methodically, asking the right questions, and iterating quickly when something didn't work.

The breadth of what I learned in a short time is something I consider one of the most significant outcomes of this experience. I didn't specialize in one narrow area — I built genuine, hands-on competence across the full stack of IT operations.

---

## Working Environment

The Quantum Campus context made this internship particularly unusual. Being embedded in an environment where the cutting edge of quantum computing research happens daily creates a different kind of professional atmosphere. The stakes around infrastructure reliability are higher. The users are highly technical. The expectations around precision, documentation, and operational discipline are elevated.

Working in this environment shaped the way I approach IT problems — with greater rigor, a stronger instinct for anticipating failure modes, and an understanding of how infrastructure work directly enables or blocks the work of researchers and engineers.

---

## Key Outcomes

By the end of the internship, I had:

- Maintained and monitored a mixed on-premises and cloud IT infrastructure serving a high-demand research campus
- Installed, configured, and maintained physical servers, virtualized environments, telephone systems, and user workstations
- Assembled computer hardware and contributed to procurement decisions for IT equipment
- Deployed and managed Docker containers and clusters in a production environment
- Produced technical documentation covering infrastructure components and operational procedures
- Operated with full autonomy across all of the above, earning and maintaining the trust of a senior team

---

## Reflection

What this internship gave me is not a list of technologies I briefly touched — it is the demonstrated ability to be dropped into a complex, real-world IT environment and produce reliable work independently. The autonomy I was given was a form of trust, and I met it.

In environments where IT teams are small and the infrastructure is critical, there is no room for someone who only operates within a narrow brief. You have to see what needs doing, figure out how to do it, and deliver. That is what I did — consistently, across every domain I was asked to cover.

The Quantum Campus context added a dimension that most IT internships don't offer: a genuine connection between infrastructure work and frontier-level research. That context made the work meaningful in a way that has stayed with me.
